Quadrivium

Welcome to My Quadrivium, or my four ways that I pursue as a matter of profession, practice, continued education, avocation, or hobby: mathematics education, philosophy, history and music. I teach college level mathematics through calculus and introductory philosophy courses. I read and do research in medieval history, history of philosophy from the ancients to early moderns, and history and philosophy of science, including mathematics. And I musically experiment with different combinations of percussion and rhythms.

The Quadrivium is a Latin term which, in the medieval Western intellectual tradition, meant the four ways of education and knowledge. The original subjects of the Quadrivium were number theory, geometry, astronomy and music. This particular group of subjects had already been outlined by Plato by the time of his writing the Republic, and they appear to have been institutionalized by Late Antiquity, if not before the collapse of the Roman Empire. The traditional Quadrivium was highly influential on the thought of many intellectuals or philosophers, from Augustine and Hypatia in Late Antiquity to Johannes Kepler by the turn of the sixteenth century.

Thinking in terms of "ways" of knowledge in such areas of mathematics is still current with some authors. For example, in The Way of Analysis, Robert Strichartz writes that "Mathematics is more than a collection of theorems, definitions, problems and techniques; it is a way of thought." The same may be said of history, which is not just a collection of names, events and dates; it is also a way of thought. Likewise for philosophy and music, or any other subject one may discover as a way to knowledge.

“By studying the process of geometric thought we may hope to arrive at what is most essential in the human mind.”
— Henry Poincare, Science and Method
